Stanford Prison Experiment:
- The Study: In 1971, Stanford University professor Philip Zimbardo conducted an experiment where 24 healthy male college students were randomly assigned to play the roles of either prisoners or guards in a simulated prison environment.
- The Results: The experiment was intended to last for two weeks, but it was terminated after just six days due to the extreme behavior of the guards and the psychological distress experienced by the prisoners. The guards began to exhibit sadistic tendencies, enforcing arbitrary rules and punishing prisoners, while the prisoners became submissive and depressed.
- The Findings: The Stanford Prison Experiment revealed the power of situational factors in shaping behavior, particularly when it comes to roles of authority and power. The study showed how quickly normal, well-adjusted individuals can become perpetrators of abuse when given power and placed in certain situations.

Regarding power posing, research by Amy Cuddy and others has shown that adopting expansive, open body language (power posing) can:
- Increase feelings of power: Power posing can increase testosterone levels and decrease cortisol levels, leading to feelings of confidence and power.
- Influence behavior: Power posing has been linked to changes in behavior, such as increased risk-taking and more assertive behavior.

In the context of the Stanford Prison Experiment, it's interesting to consider how power posing might have influenced the behavior of the guards and prisoners. For example:
- Guards' power posing: The guards' adoption of authoritarian postures and behaviors might have been reinforced by their power posing, contributing to their sadistic tendencies.
- Prisoners' submissive postures: Conversely, the prisoners' submissive postures and behaviors might have been exacerbated by their lack of power posing, contributing to their feelings of powerlessness and depression.

While the relationship between power posing and the Stanford Prison Experiment is speculative, it's clear that body language and posture can play a significant role in shaping our behavior and interactions with others.

Stanley Milgram in the 1960s. Here's a brief summary:
- The Study: Participants were recruited to take part in a study on learning and punishment. They were assigned the role of "teacher" and were told to administer electric shocks to a "learner" (an actor) every time the learner answered a question incorrectly.
- The Twist: The teacher and learner were separated by a wall, and the teacher could hear the learner's responses but not see them. The experimenter (a doctor or authority figure) instructed the teacher to increase the intensity of the shocks with each incorrect answer.
- The Results: Surprisingly, many participants were willing to administer extremely painful and even supposedly lethal shocks to the learner, simply because the experimenter told them to do so. This revealed the power of obedience to authority and the extent to which individuals might go against their own moral values when directed by someone in a position of authority.

The Milgram Experiment has been widely studied and debated, and its findings have implications for our understanding of human behavior, particularly in situations where individuals are faced with conflicting moral and authority-driven demands.
