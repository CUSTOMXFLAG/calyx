  Despite being an innovative and liberating technology, the bicycle has often been overlooked as a subject in major creative works. Unlike enclosed vehicles, cycling offers a unique perspective and experience, allowing riders to connect with their surroundings in a way that's both exhilarating and inspiring. Our goal is to challenge this perception and inspire artists, creators, and riders to celebrate the bicycle's beauty, functionality, and the ideas it sparks. By highlighting the bicycle's significance and showcasing its place in art and culture, we hope to rekindle appreciation for this remarkable invention and the joy it brings to those who ride.

Saul Steinberg's Illustrations

Saul Steinberg incorporated bicycles into his artwork as part of his broader commentary on modern life, using them in famous illustrations like the one featuring a cat riding a bicycle over a bridge in "March to April" (1966). Bicycles also appear in his work as individual motifs, sometimes in series like the Drawn Blank series, reflecting his definition of drawing as a way of reasoning on paper. Steinberg even had his own bicycle and was photographed with it, though the extent of his personal connection to cycling is not detailed. 

Artistic Representations of Bicycles



  "March to April" (1966):

  This color illustration is a notable example of Steinberg using a bicycle to create a whimsical scene. 


Drawn Blank Series:

The series includes works like the large gouache sketch "Bicycle," where the motif is explored in different artistic contexts. 
Motif in his Work:

Bicycles serve as recurring symbols in his drawings, which often explore the semantics of art and modern life. 


Steinberg's Connection to Bicycles



  Bicycles as a Tool for Reasoning:

  Steinberg viewed drawing as a way to "reason on paper," and bicycles are one of the elements he used in this process, alongside other forms like umbrellas. 


Personal Photographs:

Images of Steinberg with his bicycle exist, placing him personally within the world he depicted in his art. 


In essence, bicycles were an element within Steinberg's expansive artistic vocabulary, allowing him to comment on themes of modernity and the human experience through his unique style.

Ai Weiwei's Bicycle Installations

images:
https://twistedsifter.com/2013/10/ai-weiwei-forever-bicycles-installation-toronto/

Ai Weiwei's bicycle installations, primarily the series known as Forever Bicycles, are large-scale sculptures made from thousands of interconnected bicycle frames. The installations use the common Chinese bicycle brand, Forever, to symbolize transportation, freedom, and the changing social landscape in China and globally. These monumental works are often displayed in public spaces, creating visually complex, labyrinth-like structures. 

Key Aspects



  Materials:

  The sculptures are composed of identical bicycle frames from the "Forever" brand, a popular and accessible bicycle in China during Ai Weiwei's youth. 


Symbolism:



  Freedom and Mobility: Bicycles represent freedom of movement and the ability for individuals to navigate their world, reflecting both the past limitations and evolving freedoms in China. 


Social Change: The immense scale and optical illusions of the interconnected bicycles create a mesmerizing, evolving visual space that symbolizes the dynamic and often overwhelming changes in China's social environment. 
Commemoration: The "Forever" brand also serves as a tribute to this era and the role bicycles played in China's history, a time when they were financially out of reach for many. 


Installation:

The large sculptures are constructed in modules and assembled on-site, creating structures that can be 300 feet long and 30 feet wide. 
Purpose:

Ai Weiwei is known for his activism and for challenging authoritarianism through his art, and these bicycle installations often serve as a way to provoke thought and conversation about freedom and societal transformation. 


Notable Works



  Forever Bicycles (Taipei, 2011):

  The first version of the work, which appeared in 2011. 


Forever Bicycles (Toronto, 2013):

A 3,144-bicycle installation at Nathan Phillips Square for the Nuit Blanche art festival. 
Forever Bicycles (Winnipeg, 2019):

A version featuring 1,254 bicycles was loaned to The Forks for three years, a donation from businessman Michael Nesbitt. 
Forever Bicycles (Winnipeg, 2024):

A new loan brought the installation back to Winnipeg, where it was reassembled overlooking the Canadian Museum for Human Rights. 

Eduardo Paolozzi's Art

IMAGES:
https://www.nationalgalleries.org/art-and-artists/345


Eduardo Paolozzi, a pioneer of Pop Art, frequently incorporated bicycle parts and industrial machine elements into his art, which he explored through various media

. Instead of simply painting or sculpting a bicycle, he used its components to deconstruct and reassemble his artwork, reflecting his fascination with the intersection of technology and mass culture.

Examples of Paolozzi's bicycle art



  His Majesty the Wheel (1957): This assemblage sculpture features modular machine and bicycle components pressed into clay and then cast in bronze using the lost wax technique. The surface of the work is scarred with the impressions of cogs, nuts, and chains, with the final result appearing less as a human figure and more as a construction of mechanical parts.

  Cyclops (1957): This bronze sculpture, held in the Tate collection, showcases Paolozzi's technique of using impressions of gears, wheels, and machines to create scarred and textured surfaces on his figures.

  Textile designs (1950s): During his time teaching textile design at the Central School of Art and Design, Paolozzi created colorful, playful fabric patterns. These dizzying, repeated prints incorporated everyday objects like bicycle wheels, baked beans, and squiggles.

  The "Bunk!" collages (1947–1952): In this series of influential pop art collages, Paolozzi used found images from American magazines, including visuals of technology and machines. While not exclusively focused on bicycles, this work showcases his use of vernacular culture, which inspired his machine-like bronze works. 


The process behind the art

In the 1950s, Paolozzi developed a unique process for his bronze sculptures. He would: 


  Press found objects, such as bicycle chains, cogs, and machine parts, into slabs of clay.

  Pour wax into the imprints left in the clay.

  Cut, tear, and reassemble the wax sheets into new, fragile assemblages.

  Cast the final assembly in bronze, with the impressions of the objects now textured onto the metal surface. 
 

Photography from the "BIcycle Craze"

Photography from the bicycle craze period (primarily the 1890s) captured the social and technological changes brought by the popularity of the "safety bicycle," including images of women cyclists embracing new freedoms and fashion (like bloomers), mens' cycling clubs, outdoor races, and the use of bicycles for recreation and transportation. The era saw the simultaneous rise of the "hand camera" making photography more accessible, leading to more amateur photographers documenting the craze. 
Key Themes and Subjects in Photography:

  Women's Emancipation:

  Photos often show women riding, sometimes without chaperones, demonstrating their new independence and challenging traditional roles. 

Fashion and Dress Reform:

Images feature women in practical cycling attire, such as bloomers and shorter skirts, which were a significant departure from Victorian fashion. 
Cycling Culture and Clubs:

Photographs document the organized aspect of the craze, including bicycle club outings, races, and meetings in urban and rural settings. 
New Technology and Infrastructure:

The safety bicycle, which replaced the high-wheeled "penny-farthing," was central to these images, as were photos of bicycles on new road networks and at races on banked velodromes. 
Outdoor Recreation:

Cyclists were often photographed in picturesque natural settings, reflecting the bicycle's role in the growing outdoor recreation movement. 
Everyday Life and Urban Scenes:

Pictures captured the general public using bicycles for transportation and daily activities, such as cyclists navigating city streets. 

The Photography Itself:

  The Hand Camera's Role:

  The popularization of the Kodak and other hand cameras in the 1890s made photography easier and more widely accessible, contributing to the visual record of the bicycle craze. 

Diverse Settings:

Images range from formal composite photographs of cycling clubs to casual snapshots of individuals and groups on country roads and city streets. 
Symbolism:

Photographs were used to promote the ideals of the bicycle craze, showcasing freedom, health, and the "poetry of motion".

The Cyclist's Anthology is a classic collection of literary works centered around cycling. It was first published during the time of the "Bicycle Craze", . One notable piece featured in this anthology is "Mulga Bill's Bicycle" by A.B. 'Banjo' Paterson, first published in 1896. This poem showcases Paterson's Australian bush poet style and humorously recounts the adventures of Mulga Bill as he takes up cycling. The poem humorously recounts his adventures and mishaps as he learns to ride a bicycle, showcasing Paterson's signature style of storytelling and humor.

: It features a range of literary pieces, including poems and short stories, that celebrate cycling and explore its themes.
-The anthology offers a glimpse into the evolution of cycling as a mode of transportation and recreation during the late 19th and early 20th centuries.¹

Context and Reception:

The Cyclist's Anthology was published in a time when cycling was gaining popularity as a recreational activity during the bicycle craze. The anthology's success can be attributed to its curated selection of works that showcase the joy and challenges of cycling. While specific details about the anthology's commercial success are limited, its enduring popularity among cycling enthusiasts and literary scholars suggests that it has had a lasting impact.

Other Notable Works:

- "Tandem Trouble" by Jerome K. Jerome (1914): This humorous piece highlights the challenges and joys of tandem cycling.

THe Rider

The Rider is an autobiography of the author, following his experience in the 1977 Tour de Mont Aigoual, a 137-kilometer race in France. The Rider is considered a classic in bicycle literature.

WIND BREAKER

"Wind Breaker" is actually a manhwa, a type of Korean comic or graphic novel, that focuses on street bicycle racing and the underground cycling culture in Korea. The story follows Jo "Jay" Ja-hyeon, a high school student and skilled cyclist, as he navigates the world of extreme biking and competes in high-stakes underground racing.

WHEELING THROUGH TORONTO

"Wheeling Through Toronto: A History of the Bicycle and Its Riders" is a book written by Albert Koehl that chronicles the 130-year history of cycling in Toronto, from the bicycle craze of the 1890s to the present day. The book explores how the perception of bicycles has fluctuated over time, from being seen as fashionable and a symbol of freedom to being derided and viewed as a child's toy.

Key Themes:

- Cycling History: The book delves into Toronto's cycling past, highlighting key events, figures and infrastructure developments that have shaped the city's cycling landscape.
- Advocacy and Challenges: Koehl discusses the struggles faced by cycling advocates, including the fight for bike lanes, road safety and recognition of cycling as a legitimate mode of transportation.
- Climate Change and Sustainability: The book emphasizes the importance of cycling as a sustainable and environmentally friendly mode of transportation, particularly in the context of the climate crisis.

About the Author:

Albert Koehl is a Toronto lawyer and leading cycling advocate with over 30 years of experience in environmental law and transportation policy. He has been recognized for his work in promoting cycling and road safety in Toronto.¹ ²

Albert Einstein was known to be an avid cyclist. He often used his bicycle as a mode of transportation and found it to be a great way to clear his mind and think about complex problems. Einstein's love for cycling was not just about physical exercise but also about mental clarity.

Einstein's Cycling Habits:

- Daily Commute: Einstein would often ride his bicycle to work at Princeton University.
- Thinking on the Bike: He believed that cycling helped him think more clearly and come up with new ideas.

Einstein's Thoughts on Bicycles:

While there aren't many direct quotes from Einstein about bicycles, his love for cycling and its impact on his thought process is well-documented. He saw cycling as a way to escape the pressures of his work and find inspiration.

Legacy:

Einstein's legacy extends beyond his scientific contributions, and his love for cycling is a testament to the importance of finding balance and inspiration in everyday activities.
